Turbulence Model

k-ε Turbulence Model

Two-equation RANS model for turbulent kinetic energy and dissipation rate

\frac{\partial k}{\partial t} + U_j \frac{\partial k}{\partial x_j} = P_k - \varepsilon + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}\left[\left(\nu + \frac{\nu_t}{\sigma_k}\right)\frac{\partial k}{\partial x_j}\right]
\frac{\partial \varepsilon}{\partial t} + U_j \frac{\partial \varepsilon}{\partial x_j} = C_{1\varepsilon}\frac{\varepsilon}{k}P_k - C_{2\varepsilon}\frac{\varepsilon^2}{k} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}\left[\left(\nu + \frac{\nu_t}{\sigma_\varepsilon}\right)\frac{\partial \varepsilon}{\partial x_j}\right]

Most widely used RANS model in commercial CFD codes

What Each Variable Means

k

Turbulent Kinetic Energy

m²/s²

Energy stored in turbulent velocity fluctuations. Measures how "energetic" the turbulence is.

Typical: 0.01-10 m²/s² (low to high turbulence)

ε

Dissipation Rate

m²/s³

Rate at which turbulent kinetic energy is converted to heat by viscous action

Typical: 0.001-1000 m²/s³ (depends on turbulence intensity and length scale)

Pk

Production Term

m²/s³

Rate of turbulent kinetic energy generation by mean flow shear

Formula: Pk = μt(∂Ui/∂xj)(∂Ui/∂xj + ∂Uj/∂xi)

μt

Turbulent Viscosity

kg/(m·s)

Effective viscosity due to turbulent mixing

Formula: μt = ρCμk²/ε, where Cμ = 0.09

⚡ Think Like a Turbulence Engineer

The k-ε model treats turbulence like a energy cascade in a hydraulic system. Large eddies break down into smaller ones, eventually dissipating as heat through viscous action - like a waterfall breaking into smaller drops.

1

Energy Production (Pk)

Mean flow shear creates large turbulent eddies, like rocks in a stream creating vortices. The stronger the shear, the more turbulent energy is generated.

2

Energy Storage (k)

Turbulent kinetic energy is stored in velocity fluctuations, like kinetic energy in swirling water. This energy enhances mixing and momentum transport.

3

Energy Dissipation (ε)

Small eddies dissipate energy as heat through viscous action, like friction in hydraulic systems. Higher ε means faster energy decay.

🔧 CFD Insight: The model assumes local equilibrium between production and dissipation in the inertial subrange, making it computationally efficient but less accurate for complex flows.

Practical CFD Guidance

🔧 When to Use k-ε Model

✅ Good For

  • High Reynolds number flows (Re > 10⁵)
  • Fully developed turbulent regions
  • Industrial applications with complex geometry
  • Initial design calculations
  • Flows with mild pressure gradients
  • Computational efficiency is important

❌ Avoid For

  • Near-wall flows (y+ < 30)
  • Separated flows with recirculation
  • Swirling flows with strong rotation
  • Transitional flows (Re ~ 10³-10⁴)
  • Flows with strong streamline curvature
  • Impinging jets

🎯 CFD Setup Tips

y+ Range:

30 < y+ < 300 (use wall functions)

Inlet Turbulence:

I = 1-10%, L = 0.07×Dh

Convergence:

Monitor k and ε residuals

When k-ε Model Fails

The k-ε model has known limitations in certain flow situations. Here's when it struggles:

🌀 Near-Wall Regions

Poor prediction of near-wall turbulence due to low-Re modifications needed. ε equation becomes singular at walls. Use k-ω or wall functions instead.

🌪️ Separated Flows

Overpredicts turbulent viscosity in adverse pressure gradients, delaying separation. Poor for backward-facing steps, airfoil stall, diffuser flows.

🌊 Swirling Flows

Cannot capture streamline curvature effects or rotation-induced turbulence modification. Needs rotation/curvature corrections or Reynolds stress models.

💨 Impinging Jets

Underpredicts heat transfer and pressure recovery in stagnation regions. The standard model struggles with rapid strain rate changes.

⚖️ Buoyant Flows

Standard model doesn't account for buoyancy effects on turbulence. Needs additional source terms or specialized variants.

🔄 Transitional Flows

Cannot predict laminar-to-turbulent transition naturally. Assumes fully turbulent flow everywhere with non-zero k and ε.
